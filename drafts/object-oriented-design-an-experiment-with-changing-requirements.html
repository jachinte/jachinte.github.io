Object oriented design: an experiment with changing requirements

software engineering May 03, 2017

I've been reading a lot about object oriented programming lately. Every time I read someone else's code (and mine, of course), I can't avoid imagining a better way to organize it. I see static methods everywhere, getters and setters, null comparisons, gigantic utility classes, and whatnot. However evil are those elements considered, that's what I learnt during my bachelor;

User story

Our users are interested in improving their dietary habits. For example, some of them have been diagnosed with heart diseases, and they need to reduce fat and sugar intake, but they also need to increase sodium and something. Some market research we did in January tells us that third part of our user base would be willing to try our recipe recommendation service. What we want is to allow each user to set dietary goals, so that our recommendation engine takes that information as input, and helps users eat healthier.

Your job is to design the domain objects for implementing the dietary goals. Keep in mind that the system should be able to know if certain goal is met or not, according to the current user's health indicators.

Update 1

It's been three weeks since we released the beta version of our service. We've noticed that some of our users neither decreased nor increased some of their health indicators. What we want to do now is to detect when an indicator is maintained over time; this allows the system to take proactive action to incentivize the user to improve her health. This also comes in handy for setting a goal to maintain weight, which is something we've been hearing from our users.

This shouldn't modify your design too much, right?

Update 2

Carly and George [from marketing] have been working with focus groups since our last release. From the report they presented last week, we learnt that motivation plays a huge role in not only setting the dietary goals, but also following them. So, we decided to include the concept of policy compliance; that is, a goal is said to be met according to the policy compliance. So far we've thought of hard and soft goals, meaning that a goal is met exactly as it is expected, or that it is met within a given tolerance, respectively.

Update 3

Some of the folks at the electronics department are already working on integrating some sensors, so that users can track their health indicators. So far, they've developed a weighing scale, and a glukometer. You're required to modify your design to determine whether or not a goal is met, by requesting each device a measurement of the corresponding indicator. We'd like to be able to add new devices as the team develops more sensors.
